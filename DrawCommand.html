Systems

DrawCommand is an instruction generator which combines my fine art practice with a computer programme. It is a route to explore how I can interact with a computer, and how system art (whether in computational or fine art form) can be broken into and altered. The pursuit of eradicating the artist was central to my fine art practice. Before I knew much of anything about computer art, I was progamming myself. I broke drawing down into component parts, these component parts have been used to construct the DrawCommand programme. It is both a reflection on my practice and an exploration of how artists can interact with a computer. Rather than distancing themselves from the creative process by only making the initial decisions and allowing the programme or the process to construct the work. My previous practice was already an aberration on systems art in that I physically interacted with the system. The wobbles and movements of myself were highlighted by the process used to construct the work. DrawCommand is a translation of this into computer format. And structures how it is I could interact with a computer.

Observations

I thought that I would greatly dislike working with the computer in this way. While I found the translation of my system for making art to the computer conceptually rewarding, I thought that I would find the process clunky and uncomfortable as I had found painting with the projector in Imitation of Life. The opposite was the case. I enjoyed it, I found it quite freeing. I also immediately found that I made "mistakes" - misinterpreted the instructions, worked too fast and forgot to time myself, perhaps cheated a bit with the rules (I ignored one that told me to drop graphite powder for a day for example). The question of how to stop also came up, as the stopping point in a system is a key question of the aesthetics of construction.

Next steps

The next steps are to explore the interaction further between myself and the computer. What complexity can arise to break into the system of construction further? This is potentially where the computer needs to look at me in some way, or know what I am doing. Or perhaps undertake parallel work. What would it produce if it drew what I have drawn. It would be entirely different in aesthetics. Or could the computer tell me what to do in a more complex way? This programme is important as it is from this point that I see the development of some kind of "intelligent" computer artist.
