5.11.18 V&A visit and response to artists

We went to Chance and Control: Art in the Age of Computers at the V&A this week. I was really taken by the use of pen plotters/adapted machinery to make the works. The works on show highlighted to me the intentions of system art went into computer art (and died in fine art?). After the show I researched further into some of the artists in more detail and I have been struck by the common attraction to chaos/order, discovery, and randomness - reading their websites, I frequently identify with their pursuits though they have different approaches.

Roman Verostko is one artist who I have been drawn to for a long time. Visually, his work is highly influenced by Chinese art so it is not surprising I would gravitate towards it on that level initially. In his artist statement he says that his work is rooted in the art of early 20th century artists such as Malevich and Mondrian who sought to create art of pure form, as is mine. He sees his computer coding routines as his score for drawing. And since he discovered the use of algorithms to generate form in 1970, he has worked on developing a program of form-generators. He pursues form that could never be visualised without computing power and that have a visual life of their own. While this pursuit would have aligned with my own fine art pursuits several years ago, it increasingly does not. Having come to the conclusion that the artist must exist and the system cannot usurp the artist, why not have both the system and the artist exist and work (or merge?) together. In a way, this is what I was doing in my 20s when I was physically carrying out the system I had made rather than someone else drawing it, for example. The system actually served as an entry point for me to interact with the work. I knew this, and I was also still enjoying the game of eradicating the artist.

By 1982 Verostko was working with art-form generators so he could explore visual possibilities, make choices, refine forms, and compose a procedure for creating art which were then drawn by a pen plotter. Essentially, the machine makes the art as an art-making tool and frees the artist up to make decisions, his description of an interactive routine in which the routine pauses the machine and the paint brush is loaded by the artist. In this way the artist is almost the tool.
