DRAFT 10.10.18 Reflection on DrawBots and possible future directions

I started the year by looking at the DrawBots on the floor of my studio where I had left them a few weeks before, surrounded by paint drops from other works I have made. I work a lot on my studio floor and the DrawBots belong there, in use and integrated into the things I love. Looking at them there also started me thinking about the aesthetics of technology or the aesthetics of the future, if that is the same thing. Paint drops are not a thing normally associated with circuits and a robot. Paint drops are fundamentally human, physical, potentially intended but not controlled. Technology is contained, controlled, and has an intended purpose. Technology would not usually be compatible with a paint-speckled studio floor.

The aesthetics of technology lacks any evidence of the human hand, and, by extension, human presence. The materials are plastic or metal, and the edges and shapes regular, maybe there are lights, maybe there are pixels. My DrawBots in contrast were covered in paint and my fingerprints likely imprinted in the paint. In reality, this is more representative of how items of technology, including some (or many?) commercial ones, have actually been made. Having not made a circuit or soldered since I was 14, I had not been aware of how handmade technology can be until I made the DrawBots. Making the fairly complicated and compact circuits for the DrawBots required dexterity and skill. The DrawBots are as handmade as a painting or traditional craft in some ways - they required physical endurance like making some of my drawings can require, unlike a drawing they even caused me some pain from a shock and soldering while making them. They are hybrids in some ways - my hands and effort mixed with 3D printing and circuits. Though is that really different from a paintbrush in my hand and acrylic paint? The presentation or perception is that technology (the future?) has no human hand, apparently, however, the opposite is true. The aesthetics and potentially the general perception of technology is kind of bullshit.

I have also been reflecting on the time the DrawBots were on show. The frequency with which people humanised the robots was quite interesting. Numerous times the DrawBots were called adorable or cute. While making with them and working with them, I naturally began to humanise them myself. My DrawBots had names: Bob was my favourite because he was reliable and just kept going, Fredricka was a bit temperamental. While they were running, I worried about them and frequently kept an eye on them even when they did not need it. Is there an equivalent word for anthropomorphic for robots/technology? Or for attachment to an electronic object?

Ideas

1. On the one hand it would be good to develop the DrawBots either away from being on a wire (on the floor, or vacuumed on to the wall, for example) or develop the fact that they are wire-bound either in terms of creating a grid or spider-web formation and perhaps more complex drawing appendages. Alternatively, I could develop some weird drawing machine version of a one-man band.

2. I could explore the idea of balancing further through gyroscopes and combine kinetics with electronics.

3. It could be interesting to look through weird patents or research failed inventions and perhaps go off in a different direction.

4. Related to this is make a drawing machine based on older technology such as a mimeograph.

5. Again, somewhat related, explore sound and vibrations such as the eerie vibrations from icebergs and/or perhaps make a drawing/vibration machine by adapting older technology such as cylinder phonographs or even a CD player or tape player. Or build/adapt some kind of seismometer and record/draw vibrations such as in the city.

6. I continue to like the idea of a group of rolling balls that follows people around in a gallery space, mildly harassing them/amusing them. The physics of making a ball roll from a static position could be quite interesting especially if the shapes are slightly irregular (almost like rolling stones ... pun intended), powering them would probably use my experience from the DrawBots, and how they follow or interact with people would be a new area for me. Some examples here and here.

7. Last but not least, do what has been circulating in my mind in some form for ten years and create a parallel artist to myself and interact with it.

Perhaps also some thoughts on directions for my work I have been considering the work I want to make this year further. I am going to start with the obvious one which is to develop the code that runs the DrawBots further so they start gaining more independence from me. DRAFT 22.10.18 Systems art - reflecting on my (old) fine art practice

I have been continuing to reflect on my fine art practice in light of the DrawBots and the new skills and technology I have learned in the past year. My fine art practice has almost haunted me since I did my MA Fine Art. I was unable (even if I had been willing) to let go of my art practice. However, I had become bored by fine art materials which also struck me as failing to explore the materials of the times. I was also really put off by the narrowness and restrictions of the business of art which traded the names of artists like shares in a company whilst touting the novelty of the creativity and the bullshit of “emerging” artists. It reminded me of one of the reasons I became an accountant: independence and freedom.

In an artist statement from several years ago I said that restrictions direct my action and reduce the influence of intuition to initial decisions. Each work is an experiment bound by a system that results in the unexpected. An initial idea with the DrawBots was that they could materially represent elements of my fine art practice, for example, one could set time, one could be a certain material, another one might determine a certain movement and so these various components would come together to make system art as I used to make art. I wanted to explore the limitation of artistic intuition, and, therefore, the displacement of the artist, through computation. I had increasingly come to regard systems art as a game that could not be won. The reality of the DrawBots was that they emphasised the impossibility of game I have been playing. In my fine art practice, I tried to reduce myself to physical involvement as much as possible and I ended up refining (or highlighting) my involvement in what I called accidents. The blobs, the smears, the pen smudges. The life. The creativity. The not a robot.

With the DrawBots, I did not want to leave them alone, I played with them. I explored them. I put paint on them. I tried ink. I experimented. This resulted in two things: it made me interested in what it would take for them to become more of an equal than a tool; and it highlighted what I already knew: systems art resulting in the eradication of the artist such as I had been pursuing several years ago was not possible but there was much more to explore around the presence and absence of the artist through systems and by bringing computational and artist systems together. Rather than the artist (or human) being eradicated by some system (computational or otherwise), it could be explored, developed, brought together with computation and technology.

29.10.18 DrawBots and Line Follower

I have been working on switch statements to create a pretend-intelligence for the DrawBots to develop them from being in the area of mechanical tools to progressing them towards being more my parallel. My intention is to see whether they feel more like my equal or remain tools when I actually work with slightly more complicated behaviour. I am also exploring technically how to imitate intelligence (or something that seems like intelligence). Presumably creating complexity imitates intelligence.

In conjunction with this I am also exploring line following robots. This is with the idea of taking the group communication and behaviour from the DrawBots and merging this with line following robots to create a group of line drawing and following robots that move along the floor that I interact with and draw with too. This could be translated to vacuum robots vertically on the wall. This is an alternative development of merging and developing the concepts of systems art so the idea (or system) is not just the machine that makes the art (Sol LeWitt).
