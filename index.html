Sketches with a projector

These three sketches represent my first experiments interacting with a computer to create art. I projected Processing sketches on to my studio wall to sketch on paper with them. I was immediately uncomfortable and self-conscious.

The speed at which the computer could physically operate, along with its accuracy, far outstripped my speed and accuracy. I couldn’t even improvise fast enough. The computer was not only faster, it drew straight lines and perfect circles repeatedly. I became frustrated and it did not. I also found that the way in which the computer produced images using light was not compatible for me as the artist working in a bright rectangle in a dark studio. I couldn’t see properly, I could not choose my colours properly. We existed in physically very different ways. The computer in this context was light, speed, accuracy, flatness and, in contrast, I was physical, required touch to convey my presence, was slower, and had feelings (such as discomfort).

As a result of these sketches I also realised that there were differences in terms of aesthetics and graphics which would make working with a computer in this way complicated. I made marks with gestures using my finger, the computer drew shapes and graphics. I wobbled, whereas the computer made perfect edges. A computer has no touch, and if it does, it's touch is a flickering loop of light in this case. Even the materials I used were not compatible - paint does not emit light.

Imitation of Life

These drawings are a visual variation of a conceptual system. They are an extension of a direction my fine art practice was taking in which I drew the boundaries of the system which then acted as a physical target, rather than conceiving of a set of rules that I carried out. At the time, I questioned and was uncertain about the fact that I was physically drawing the target that I was then aiming at because it potentially became an aesthetic effect of opposite graphic elements (a ruler drawn line and a splash of paint for example) rather than the result of something more fundamental.

The drawings were made using Processing sketches which were printed off and acted as a structure I then followed with a pen. They are part of my work considering how to both physically and graphically work with something that exists in such a different form to myself. The work was also set up to explore how I felt working with printed, static sketches versus moving sketches given my level of discomfort working with the projected sketches. It was a much more satisfying and more calm experience working with printed sketches rather than light projections in a dark studio moving at speed.

The line drawing starkly sets out the fact I am alive unlike the computer - my breath, the physical position I was drawing in, my discomfort and shifting, my inaccuracies and the lapses in my attention as my mind wandered. The computer printed sketch has no movement, it is uniform, it has no wobbles or breath or thought to distract the movement of the line. Visually, the difference between the two is stark. I considered what it would take for the computer line to look like my line, and I decided to bring in random and the idea of scribbles to see whether that would bring the appearance of the marks closer together. Visually, it is more difficult to tell my hand apart from the computer “hand” in the scribbles sketches. It also raises the consideration that the multitude of lines in addition to random creates visual complexity which obscures my ability as the viewer and the artist to differentiate between the two hands.

Random and the hand

These images represent a combination of computer sketches, hand made marks, and unintentional marks on the walls of my studio and paper which have resulted from the making of other works. This forms part of my thinking around how to bring my hand and the computer hand closer together, and what they are like visually as graphic opposites.
